🚀 Sam Altman: "I Felt Terrible." OpenAI's CEO Just Gave the Most Unfiltered Interview of His Career

🎥 https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mJSnn0GZmls

Laurie Segall sat Sam Altman down at his home for his first interview since killing Sora and signing the Pentagon deal. What followed was 60+ minutes of rare, unscripted honesty you won't find in a press release.

Here's what he actually said:

⸻

Key Takeaways

1️⃣ Sora Is Dead — And It's All About Compute 🖥️
"It's all about compute... before the compute crunch came, we were talking about putting it into the ChatGPT app… but one thing we realized is that would have put a series of incentives on us and would've led to a bunch of decisions to win that we just didn't want to make." OpenAI needed to "concentrate our compute and our product capacity into these next generation of automated researchers and companies." Even the most beloved products get cut when resources are finite — and OpenAI is betting everything on AI agents over video.

2️⃣ "I Felt Terrible" — The Disney Call That Haunts Him 🎬
Altman personally called Disney CEO Josh D'Amaro to break the news about Sora's shutdown. "The very first thing that the new Disney CEO Josh said to me, and I felt, like, terrible… He's like, 'I get it.' But it's super sad always to disappoint a partner or users or a team, all of which are doing incredible work." He also added: "I love Sora. I love generated videos and I love our partnership with Disney, and we're working hard with them to find a world where they can still do something amazing." Despite the fallout, OpenAI and Disney are reportedly still in talks.

3️⃣ Pentagon Deal: "I Was Miscalibrated" 🏛️
On OpenAI's controversial Department of War partnership, Altman made a candid admission: "Are AI companies or are governments more powerful? And I think it's very important that the governments are more powerful… We have to work with government, but the intensity of the current mood of mistrust, I was miscalibrated on." This is rare — a tech CEO publicly acknowledging he underestimated public concern about AI-military ties, and doubling down on the principle that governments must stay more powerful than AI companies.

4️⃣ The One-Person Billion-Dollar Company Already Exists 💰
Altman dropped a bombshell: the first solo-founder, billion-dollar AI-powered company is already real. "It is a legitimate single person… a massively successful company with one person." This signals we've crossed a historic threshold — what once required entire departments, seed rounds, and teams of engineers can now be built by a single person leveraging AI agents. The economics of entrepreneurship have been rewritten.

5️⃣ A Decade of Science in One Year 🔬
Altman described a near-term future where automated AI researchers could compress a decade of scientific discovery into a single year, fundamentally reshaping innovation. Cures for diseases, climate breakthroughs, and technological leaps that previously took generations could arrive within years. This may be the most consequential — and underreported — prediction in the entire interview.

6️⃣ AI Job Disruption Is Coming — And He Admits It ⚠️
Altman acknowledged that AI will lead to "a tremendous amount of job loss" and that the near-term period will be disruptive, calling for entirely new economic models to support workers through the transition. He also revealed what he considers the AI-proof jobs of the future. And the one word he says defines his entire life's work? "Abundance."

7️⃣ His Own Son Won't Touch AI — Yet 👶
The CEO of OpenAI said he'd "rather be on the late end of what's reasonable" before introducing his son to AI. "He'll grow up in a world where computers are smarter than him and do anything he wants, but… I wanted him to play in the dirt for now." When Segall pushed back — pointing out that Altman is essentially "raising her child too" because his technology shapes every child's future — a real, unscripted tension broke through the interview.

8️⃣ The iPad Trap — And How He's Avoiding It 📱
Altman expressed deep concern about tech addiction in children, saying "when I watch kids just a little bit older than mine that you cannot take the iPad away from," he feels "very strongly" about keeping screens away from his son. The irony? The same man building the most powerful AI in history also said he "cannot imagine" raising a newborn without ChatGPT. The contradiction is striking — and completely unresolved.

9️⃣ AI in Hollywood: "More Care for Human Creators, Not Less" 🎭
On AI's impact on entertainment, Altman pushed back on fears of creative displacement, stating that AI will get people to "care more about human creators, not less." He envisions a future where AI amplifies rather than replaces human artistry — though given the Sora shutdown and the Disney drama, Segall made clear the tension between that vision and today's reality is far from settled.

🔟 "Tough Resourcing Calls" — The Real Cost of Being CEO 🎯
Altman offered a rare, unfiltered look at the psychological weight of running the world's most consequential AI company. "There are like many hard parts about being a CEO that you don't get sympathy for… but one of them is, like, you have to like make a lot of like very tough resourcing calls and a lot of good things get caught up in that because they're not the most important thing." It's a reminder that even at the summit of tech power, nothing about this is easy.

⸻

Final Thought

💡 Sam Altman just gave the most human interview of his career — and what emerged was a portrait of a man trying to hold simultaneous truths: building the most powerful technology in history while worrying about iPad addiction in toddlers, declaring AI will create abundance while admitting job disruption is real, championing government oversight while navigating Pentagon deals. Whether you trust him or not, this conversation is essential listening for anyone trying to understand where we're headed.
