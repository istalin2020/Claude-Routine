🚀 Jensen Huang: "Run, Don't Walk" — NVIDIA CEO's Battle Cry to the Class of 2026

🎥 https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FZh_0uRgrg4

In a 20-minute keynote now ricocheting across tech Twitter, NVIDIA's founder told 5,800 CMU graduates they're entering the workforce at the start of the biggest computing reset in 60 years. The kicker? He says the AI boom's biggest winners aren't software engineers — they're plumbers, electricians, and welders.

Here's what he actually said:

⸻

Key Takeaways

1️⃣ Run, Don't Walk Toward AI 🏃‍♂️
"So run, don't walk." Huang's central call to action was blunt and immediately viral — there's no time to drift in this moment. He told graduates this is "a once-in-a-generation opportunity" and the worst possible response is to wait it out on the sidelines.

2️⃣ Computing Just Hit a Hard Reset 🔄
"Computing is undergoing a complete reset. For 60 years, computing worked the same way. Humans wrote software, computers executed instructions. That paradigm is over." Huang framed AI not as another tool layered on top of the old stack, but as the end of the era we've lived in since the 1960s — and the start of a wholly new one where machines learn instead of follow instructions.

3️⃣ AI Won't Replace You — Someone Using AI Will 🤖
"AI does not replace human purpose — it amplifies human capability." Radiologists will read more scans and spend more time with patients; engineers will tackle bigger problems while AI handles the routine boilerplate. The line graduates are already sharing online: "It's not AI, but those who use AI better who will replace you."

4️⃣ The Real Winners? Plumbers and Electricians 🔧
"Electricians, plumbers, steelworkers, technicians, construction workers — this is your time." With demand for construction workers up 30%, welders up 25%, and electricians up 18%, Huang argued the trades will physically build the chip factories and data centers powering AI. Companies literally can't hire fast enough to replace retiring tradespeople.

5️⃣ The Biggest Buildout in Human History 🏗️
"This is the largest technology infrastructure buildout in human history and a once-in-a-generation opportunity to reindustrialize America." Huang made the case that AI isn't a pure software story — it's a physical, industrial story requiring chip fabs, data centers, and advanced manufacturing on a scale the U.S. hasn't seen in generations.

6️⃣ A First-Generation Immigrant's Pitch for America 🇺🇸
Huang shared that he was sent at age 9 to a Baptist boarding school in Oneida, Kentucky, while his father later worked as a chemical engineer and his mother as a maid. "My parents came here because they believed America could give their children a chance." It landed as a powerful counter-narrative in a moment when tech optimism feels hard to come by.

7️⃣ AI Must Belong to Everyone 🌍
"Make AI broadly accessible. Everyone should have AI. Opportunities should not belong only to the people who can code." Huang argued AI's deepest value is in democratizing capability — leveling the playing field for people without elite credentials, expensive degrees, or technical training. It's a thesis that puts NVIDIA on the side of access, not gatekeeping.

8️⃣ Don't Fear the Future — Build It 🛠️
"The answer is not to fear the future. The answer is to guide it wisely, build it responsibly, and ensure that its benefits reach as many people as possible." It was a direct rebuke to AI doomerism and to the temptation to sit on the sidelines while the world reshapes itself. Huang's framing: agency, not anxiety.

9️⃣ We're All at the Same Starting Line 🏁
"We are all standing at the same starting line. This is your moment to help shape what comes next." With every legacy company forced to relearn how software works, Huang argued no incumbent has a real lead — a once-in-a-career window for new graduates to leapfrog 20-year veterans who built their careers on the old paradigm.

🔟 Put Your Heart in the Work ❤️
"Carnegie Mellon has a motto I love: 'My heart is in the work.' So put your heart in the work. Build something worthy of your education, your potential and the people who believed in you long before the world did." The closer earned a standing ovation — and clips of it went viral within hours of the ceremony ending.

⸻

Final Thought

💡 Huang's speech lands at a strange moment — tech layoffs are climbing, new grads are panicking about AI taking entry-level jobs, and arts students at UCF reportedly booed when AI was mentioned at their own ceremony the same weekend. CMU's grads cheered. The split says everything about who feels this revolution is happening *to* them versus who's planning to build it themselves.
